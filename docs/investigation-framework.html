Investigation and Accountability Framework

How the system maintains integrity while preserving photographer privacy and safety.

Why This Page Exists

The Birthmark Standard is designed to never need investigations. The system's security model assumes cameras are trustworthy—encrypted in secure elements, validated by manufacturers, with ~$100K-$1M attack costs making individual camera compromise economically irrational.

But we need a response plan for two scenarios:

  • State-level attacks: Nation-states with resources to compromise cameras at scale
  • Credibility attacks: Bad actors claiming "the system can't detect fakes" to undermine trust

This page documents our contingency plan—showing how we'd respond IF cameras were compromised while preserving photographer privacy and safety. Transparency about this capability is essential for Coalition members evaluating governance, privacy advocates assessing safeguards, and journalists deciding whether to trust the system.

Key point: Investigations don't change photographer threat models. Anyone can submit images to forensic analysis services today. We're documenting how the system maintains integrity while preserving privacy protections stronger than any existing authentication system.

Investigation Scope and Limitations

What Investigations CAN Reveal:

✓ Camera serial number (for blacklisting)

✓ Technical forensic evidence (noise patterns, compression artifacts)

What Investigations CANNOT Reveal:

✗ Photographer identity

✗ Image content

✗ Capture locations or times

✗ Who you shared images with

✗ Your other photographs

Cost to trigger investigation: $500 (non-refundable if rejected)

Required evidence: Professional forensic analysis showing specific technical anomalies

Governance: 3-member committee from journalism organizations (rotating 6-month terms)

The Credibility Challenge

If compromised cameras could authenticate AI-generated images indefinitely, critics could credibly claim "your system is worthless—you can't detect fakes." This isn't about likely attacks (camera compromise is expensive and technically difficult), but about maintaining system credibility in the face of determined adversaries.

Without a documented response plan, every suspicious image becomes ammunition for "the system doesn't work." With transparent safeguards, we can say: "We've thought through this scenario, here are the protections, and here's how we'd respond while preserving privacy."

How This Compares to Today

Forensic Analysis Today (Without Birthmark)

  • Anyone can submit your images to forensic services (FotoForensics, Hive Moderation, etc.)
  • No cost barriers against frivolous submissions
  • No oversight or governance
  • No appeal process
  • No consequences for false accusations
  • Results can be shared publicly without your knowledge

With Birthmark Investigation Framework

  • $500 cost barrier against frivolous claims (forfeited if rejected)
  • Professional forensic analysts (not random internet users)
  • Investigation Committee oversight (journalism organizations)
  • Formal appeal process with independent review
  • Enhanced protections for high-risk contexts (conflict zones, CPJ Index regions)
  • Limited consequences (camera serial number only, never photographer identity)
  • Sunset clause (Coalition can vote to end investigations if harmful)

Bottom line: Investigation doesn't create photographer vulnerability—it formalizes existing forensic processes with stronger safeguards and oversight by journalism organizations whose mission is protecting press freedom.

Core Safeguards

1. Investigation Committee Structure

  • 3 rotating Coalition Members handle all investigations (6-month terms, staggered rotation)
  • Prevents overwhelming all Coalition Members with investigation responsibilities
  • Organizations can plan staffing around predictable rotation schedules
  • Full Coalition override: Any Coalition Member can escalate decisions to full vote (67% supermajority required)

2. Financial Barriers Against Abuse

  • $500 claim fee to submit investigation request
  • Non-refundable if rejected at preliminary review or committee vote
  • Refunded if investigation proceeds ($500 back)
  • $500 bounty if camera blacklisted ($1,000 total return)
  • Makes frivolous claims expensive while rewarding legitimate concerns
  • Fee waiver: Any Coalition Member or Foundation board member can waive fee for legitimate concerns

3. Technical Evidence Required

Foundation contracts professional forensic analysts (organizations like Bellingcat and credentialed photographic forensics experts).

Claims must show specific technical indicators:

  • Inconsistent noise patterns measurable against sensor model specifications
  • Impossible lighting geometry (shadow directions, physics violations)
  • Metadata anomalies (timestamps, EXIF inconsistencies)
  • Compression artifacts inconsistent with claimed processing pipeline

Rejected immediately: "This looks suspicious," "lighting seems weird," "this makes our government look bad," or subjective assessments without measurable technical evidence.

Quality control: Periodic independent audits where second analyst organizations review the first analyst's work for quality and bias detection.

4. Enhanced Protections for High-Risk Contexts

Investigations involving regions on CPJ's Global Impunity Index (countries where journalists are murdered and killers go free) or active conflict zones receive:

  • Unanimous committee vote required (3 of 3, not 2 of 3)
  • Mandatory consultation with press freedom organizations (RSF, CPJ, regional groups)
  • Higher evidence threshold: Extraordinarily strong forensic indicators (multiple independent anomalies)
  • Expedited appeals: 7 days maximum (not 14 days)
  • Escalation rights: Any Coalition Member can force full Coalition vote if consultation ignored

Important: Investigation never reveals photographer identity, image content, or location—only camera serial number. If activists strip geotag metadata before sharing (which we recommend for dangerous documentation), there's no location information to investigate. The system investigates blatant fabrication (claiming false location), not omission (no location data provided).

5. Distributed Control

Transaction log decryption requires multiple parties cooperating:

  • 3 of 5 geographically distributed key holders must cooperate
  • Validator nodes rotate through key holder roles
  • Cross-jurisdiction storage: Private keys stored outside node's operating jurisdiction (US node's key in Europe, etc.)
  • Compromising one jurisdiction or organization is insufficient
  • Sequential compromise required: Must compromise first node to identify second target
  • Key rotation means attackers must succeed within limited time windows

6. Limited Consequences

  • Investigation reveals only camera serial number for blacklisting
  • Never reveals: Photographer identity, image content, capture locations
  • Historical images remain authenticated: Blacklist only blocks future submissions
  • Clean appeal records: Successful appeals completely remove blacklist with no public "reversed" notation

7. Transparency Commitment

Annual public reports with aggregate statistics:

  • Claims submitted, rejected, investigations authorized
  • Cameras blacklisted, appeals filed, appeals granted
  • Geographic distribution (by region, not specific countries)
  • Committee rotation schedules
  • Claim fee revenue and bounty payouts

Coalition oversight: Any Coalition Member can access investigation materials (prevents hidden bias)

Sunset review: Five-year recurring review with option to discontinue investigations if no longer necessary or if Coalition determines capability has become harmful

How Investigations Work

1. Claim Submission

Someone possesses an authenticated image showing signs of AI generation (impossible lighting, inconsistent noise patterns, etc.). They submit $500 claim fee with the image.

2. Foundation Preliminary Review

Professional forensic analysts contracted by Foundation conduct initial assessment using industry-standard tools. Claims without specific technical indicators get rejected immediately (fee forfeited).

3. Investigation Committee Review

Committee reviews forensic analyst findings and votes whether to proceed:

  • Standard investigations: 2 of 3 committee members required
  • High-risk contexts (CPJ Index regions, conflict zones): 3 of 3 required
  • If approved, investigation proceeds. If rejected, claimant notified and fee forfeited.

4. Transaction Log Decryption

  • Committee authorizes distributed key reconstruction
  • Requires 3 of 5 geographically distributed key holders to cooperate
  • Decrypted logs reveal encrypted camera certificate submitted to manufacturer

5. Manufacturer Identification

  • Manufacturer receives NUC hash from decrypted certificate
  • Looks up camera serial number in production database
  • Returns serial number to Investigation Committee
  • Privacy preserved: Manufacturer never sees image content, capture time, or location

6. Blacklist Decision

  • Committee votes to blacklist (2 of 3 required; 3 of 3 for high-risk contexts)
  • Public blacklist shows: Serial number, manufacturer, model, technical justification
  • Owner receives notification with appeal instructions (if contact info on file)
  • Future submissions from blacklisted camera automatically rejected

7. Appeal Process

  • Owner submits actual image files from blacklist period
  • Provides technical explanation for flagged artifacts
  • Independent review: Technical expert (not committee members who voted to blacklist) reviews evidence
  • Committee votes on appeal (2 of 3 to reverse)
  • During appeal: Owner can submit hashes of critical images for immediate authentication (coverage continues while appeal proceeds)

8. If Appeal Succeeds

  • Blacklist entry completely removed (no "reversed" notation)
  • Images from blacklist period authenticated with current timestamp
  • Future verifiers see clean authentication records
  • Internal database tracks claim history to prevent repeated frivolous reports

Frequently Asked Questions

Can investigations identify photographers?

No. Investigation reveals only camera serial number. Manufacturers may or may not have purchaser records—cameras are sold secondhand, gifted, stolen. Even with serial number and purchaser name, you cannot link specific images to specific people without already possessing those images.

The system is designed so no entity can correlate "this person took this photo" without outside information:

  • Manufacturers know camera serial numbers but not what those cameras captured
  • Coalition knows authentication records but not which cameras they came from
  • Submission Servers see image hashes but not camera identities
  • Investigation requires all three cooperating, authorized by distributed key holders, governed by journalism organizations whose mission is protecting press freedom

What protects photographers in dangerous regions?

Regions on CPJ's Global Impunity Index or active conflict zones receive enhanced protections:

  • Unanimous committee vote required (3 of 3) instead of 2 of 3
  • Mandatory consultation with press freedom organizations (RSF, CPJ, regional groups) before proceeding
  • Higher evidence threshold: Multiple independent technical anomalies required
  • Escalation rights: If consultation org objects and committee proceeds anyway, any Coalition Member can force full Coalition vote (67% required)
  • Expedited appeals: 7 days maximum instead of 14

Importantly, investigation never reveals photographer identity, image content, or location—only camera serial number. If activists strip geotag metadata before sharing (which we recommend for dangerous documentation), there's no location information to investigate.

Investigation and Editorial Freedom

Images can only be investigated if you've shared them with someone else. At that point, the risk is identical to existing forensic analysis—anyone can submit suspicious images to forensic services today. The investigation pathway doesn't change this calculus.

If you're documenting sensitive material, you already control who sees it before sharing. Investigation doesn't reveal photographer identity—it only determines whether a specific camera serial number should be blacklisted from future authentication.

If you're concerned about unusual compositions being misinterpreted, don't share those images until you're comfortable with scrutiny—exactly as you would today with existing forensic analysis capabilities.

How do you distinguish AI-generated images from unusual legitimate photography?

We use the same forensic tools professional fact-checkers use today: Hive Moderation, FotoForensics, metadata analysis, noise pattern analysis, compression artifact detection. We're not claiming special detection capability.

The difference is we only apply these tools to reported images (with cost barrier preventing spam) rather than every image. Foundation contracts professional forensic analysts who do this work for journalism organizations and fact-checking groups already. Claims must identify specific measurable anomalies, not subjective assessments like "lighting looks weird."

Examples of rejected claims:

  • "This protest makes our government look bad, must be fake" - political motivation, no technical evidence
  • "Unusual lighting" - insufficient without specific physics violations
  • "Subject didn't authorize this photo" - copyright issue, not authentication issue

Examples of accepted claims:

  • "Shadow directions inconsistent with reported sun position at documented time/location"
  • "Noise pattern inconsistent with sensor model specifications"
  • "Multiple re-encoding artifacts despite claim this is first-generation JPEG"

What prevents governments from flooding the system with false claims?

The $500 non-refundable claim fee. Claims rejected at Foundation preliminary review or Investigation Committee forfeit the fee. This makes frivolous claims expensive ($500 per rejected claim) while ensuring legitimate concerns aren't blocked (any Coalition Member or Foundation board member can waive the fee).

Additionally, claims must survive professional forensic analysis showing specific technical evidence before reaching the Investigation Committee. "This makes us look bad" gets rejected immediately at preliminary review, and the fee is forfeited.

Camera Blacklist Appeals

If your camera is blacklisted, you receive notification with appeal instructions. You can:

  • Submit hashes of critical images immediately: During appeal, submit hashes of images from the blacklist period and they'll be authenticated immediately while the full appeal proceeds. Your documentation coverage continues uninterrupted.
  • Provide technical explanation: Explain the artifacts that triggered investigation (e.g., "unusual lighting was from reflector at specific angle, not AI generation")
  • Submit actual image files: Foundation processes images from blacklist period as new submissions with current timestamp
  • Independent expert review: Technical expert (not the committee members who voted to blacklist) reviews your evidence
  • Committee vote on reversal: 2 of 3 required (maximum 14 days; 7 days for high-risk contexts)

If appeal succeeds, the blacklist is completely removed with no public "reversed" notation. Your images appear as normal authenticated images with clean records. Only internal database tracks claim history to prevent repeated frivolous reports on the same image.

Expedited appeals: Coalition Members can expedite appeals for photographers working in high-risk contexts. If you have relationships with organizations like WITNESS, RSF, or CPJ, they can request immediate expedited review.

Who conducts forensic analysis and investigates?

Foundation contracts professional forensic analysts (organizations like Bellingcat and credentialed experts in photographic forensics). We don't staff in-house experts. Investigation Committee members are representatives from journalism organizations—they vote on whether contracted analyst findings justify action, but they don't conduct analysis themselves.

Depending on claim throughput, we implement periodic audits where a second independent analyst organization reviews the first analyst's work for quality control and bias detection. Any Coalition Member can request access to investigation materials, ensuring transparency within Coalition.

What prevents "scope creep" - using investigations for censorship or political purposes?

The Coalition consists of journalism organizations, fact-checking groups, and press freedom advocates (NPPA, CPJ, Reporters Without Borders, IFCN, etc.) whose entire mission is protecting against those expansions. If a Coalition Member attempts to use investigations for purposes beyond technical authentication, that is explicit grounds for removal under the Governance Charter.

Additionally:

  • Claims must survive professional forensic analysis showing specific technical evidence
  • $500 fee makes politically motivated claims expensive
  • Any Coalition Member can escalate questionable committee decisions to full Coalition vote
  • Annual public reports enable external researchers to monitor for abuse patterns
  • Five-year recurring review with option to sunset investigations if Coalition determines the capability has become harmful or is no longer necessary

At some point you need to trust people. We're identifying the most trustworthy organizations possible—groups that fight surveillance and censorship for a living.

What if my camera is stolen and used to create fakes?

Blacklist cannot retroactively remove images already posted to blockchain. We can only blacklist future submissions. This means:

  • Historical documentation remains intact: Images authenticated before theft stay authenticated
  • Future submissions blocked: Prevents stolen camera from authenticating new fakes
  • Appeal available: Explain theft, prove previous legitimate use
  • Committee can note in blacklist: "Camera compromised via theft, photographer not at fault"
  • Obtain new camera: Continue documentation work with different hardware

This protects your historical work while preventing the stolen camera from causing further damage.

Can governments compel manufacturer or Coalition Members to cooperate with investigations?

Manufacturers alone are insufficient. They can identify camera serial numbers from NUC hashes, but they don't know what those cameras have captured, when, where, or by whom. You cannot link photos to people without Coalition cooperation to decrypt transaction logs.

Coalition cooperation requires compromising multiple nodes:

  • Compromise first node to identify which Submission Server received the hash
  • Compromise second node holding decryption keys for that server's logs during the time window
  • Second target is only revealed after compromising first node
  • Must succeed before key rotation moves keys to different nodes

Cross-jurisdiction protection: Private keys are stored outside node's operating jurisdiction (US node's key in Europe, European node's key in Asia), preventing single-jurisdiction government compulsion. Compromising two Coalition Members sequentially, across multiple jurisdictions, within key rotation windows, without detection is exponentially harder than single-point compromise.

Additionally, government requests trigger node removal per Governance Charter, not compliance. Governments can remove nodes from the network but cannot compel them to cooperate.

Why is this investigation capability necessary?

If compromised cameras can mint valid authentication certificates for AI-generated images, the entire system becomes worthless. Without the ability to identify and blacklist compromised cameras, a single breach could authenticate thousands of fakes before detection.

The alternative is accepting that once a camera is compromised, it can authenticate unlimited AI-generated images forever. That makes the authentication system meaningless.

We've designed the investigation pathway with maximum safeguards:

  • Distributed control (no single entity can investigate unilaterally)
  • Professional forensic analysis (not subjective determinations)
  • Financial barriers against abuse ($500 per rejected claim)
  • Enhanced protections for high-risk contexts (unanimous votes, mandatory consultation)
  • Limited consequences (only camera serial number, never photographer identity)
  • Transparency (annual public reports, Coalition oversight)
  • Sunset review (five-year recurring evaluation with option to deprecate)

The question isn't "should investigations exist?" but rather "how do we make them as safe as possible while maintaining system integrity?" This framework is our answer.

Can I observe investigations to ensure the process works as intended?

Coalition Members can observe Investigation Committee deliberations, request access to all investigation materials, and participate in post-action reviews of completed investigations.

We're committed to treating the first 3-5 investigations as pilot cases with extensive documentation and public reporting (without revealing photographer identities or image content). This allows Coalition Members and external researchers to evaluate whether "extraordinarily strong forensic evidence" is being interpreted consistently and whether mandatory consultation is functioning as intended.

If the process doesn't work as designed, Coalition Members will raise concerns and vote to adjust procedures or sunset the capability entirely.

This investigation framework exists to protect system integrity while preserving photographer privacy and safety. We've designed it with input from journalism organizations, privacy advocates, and human rights documentation groups to ensure the safeguards are meaningful.
